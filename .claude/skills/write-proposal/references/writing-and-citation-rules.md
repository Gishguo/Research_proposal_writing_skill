# Writing and Citation Rules

## Citation Rules

- Use APA in-text citation style throughout: `(Author, Year)` for narrative citations, `(Author & Author, Year)` for two authors, `(Author et al., Year)` for three or more
- Every factual claim, statistic, or methodological choice must be supported by at least one citation from the confirmed reference pool
- The professor's papers should be cited where they are genuinely relevant — only include them when they naturally support the argument, not as a formality
- Do NOT invent citations. Only use references confirmed in the reference pool
- Aim for ~20 in-text citation instances distributed across the proposal (not all in one section)

### Citation Function Categories

Organize references by function, not by source type:

| Function | Where used | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| **Context-setting** | Introduction / Background | Orient the reader; establish the field |
| **Gap-establishing** | Problem Statement, Literature Review §2.3 | Demonstrate what is not yet known |
| **Method-justifying** | Methodology | Justify why the chosen methods are appropriate |
| **Benchmark / dataset** | Methodology, Data Collection | Identify the data or baselines used |
| **Limitation-aware** | Limitations | Acknowledge known constraints of the approach |

Do not include a reference unless it serves one of these functions.

### Reference Pool Construction

Priority order:
1. User-provided professor publications (authoritative; do not supplement)
2. User-provided review or survey summaries (primary gap-identification source)
3. Targeted web searches only when gap evidence, method justification, or benchmark references are insufficient

Target breakdown: professor's papers (5–8) + foundational works (~5) + recent advances (~6) + methodological references (~4) = ~20 total.

## Language Polish Rules

Apply these rules in the final polish pass. The threshold is conservative — if the text already sounds natural and idiomatic, preserve it.

### Formality
- Use full forms only: "it is" not "it's", "does not" not "doesn't"
- Avoid possessives on method names: not "METHOD's performance" → "the performance of METHOD"

### Claim Language
- Do not introduce new claims during polishing
- Do not strengthen claims without evidence
- Do not use "formal characterization", "validated framework", "theoretical contribution", or "principled basis" unless the evidence supports it

### Anti-AI-Style Rules

Remove or rewrite:
- Mechanical transition phrases: *First and foremost*, *It is worth noting that*, *In conclusion, it can be seen that*
- Overly symmetric sentence structures that feel templated
- Hollow intensifiers: *truly*, *certainly*, *undoubtedly*, *remarkably*
- Unnatural hedging stacks: *may potentially possibly suggest*
- Generic filler openings: *In recent years*, *With the rapid development of*, *It is widely acknowledged that*

Rewrite flagged phrases using logical sentence progression rather than explicit connectives.

### Sentence Structure
- Untangle run-on sentences
- Vary sentence length — mix short declarative sentences with longer analytical ones
- Prefer active voice where it does not sound unnatural for the field

### Technical Precision
- Preserve technical meaning above all else
- Do not substitute a precise technical term with a vaguer synonym for stylistic reasons
- Ensure mathematical notation is consistent and correctly formatted

### Self-Check Before Finalizing
1. Does each sentence earn its place — does it add information or advance an argument?
2. Would a native English-speaking reviewer find any sentence awkward or unclear?
3. Is every claim in the polished text still supported by the same evidence as in the draft?
