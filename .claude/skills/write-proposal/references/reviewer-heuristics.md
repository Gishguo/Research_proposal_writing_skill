# Reviewer Heuristics

This file contains the perspective of a PhD supervisor and scholarship reviewer. Use it during Phase 2.8 (Reviewer Stress Test) to anticipate objections before drafting.

## Common Supervisor Objections

### On the Research Gap
- Is this really a research gap, or just an uncombined set of topics? Combining two existing methods is not a gap unless the combination creates a genuinely new problem or requires new theory.
- Has the gap already been addressed in a paper the user has not read? Require gap-supporting citations before accepting the gap claim.

### On the Contribution
- Is the contribution overstated? Simulation results are not a "formal theory." A design chart is not a "theoretical framework." A parameter sweep is not a "principled characterization."
- Does the proposal contain more than one primary novel contribution? If so, it is likely over-scoped. One primary contribution with supporting components is the correct structure for a PhD.
- Is a proposed new concept (index, curve, equivalence relation, metric, framework) truly theoretical, or merely a numerical planning artifact derived from simulation sweeps?

### On the Methodology
- Does the methodology actually answer the research questions? Trace each RQ to a specific method. If the link is vague, the methodology is incomplete.
- Does the data support the level of claim being made? Component-level data cannot directly support system-level claims without explicit bridging assumptions.
- Are cross-scale or cross-layer assumptions explicitly justified? If the proposal moves from cell-level to system-level, or from dispatch to annual reliability, the assumptions enabling that move must be stated and defended.

### On the Scope
- Is the scope realistic for a PhD (3–5 years)? A proposal that requires a new degradation model, a new control framework, a new reliability metric, and a multi-region generalization study is not a PhD — it is a research program.
- Is the minimum viable contribution clearly identified? What is the core result that would constitute a defensible PhD even if extensions are not completed?

### On Substitutability and Equivalence Claims
- Is a substitutability or equivalence claim decomposed into defensible dimensions? "A can substitute for B" is not a research claim. "A can substitute for B in dimension X under conditions Y, but not in dimension Z" is.
- Does the substitution saturate? At what point does the substitution break down?

### On Candidate and Supervisor Fit
- Does the proposal match the applicant's background? Can the applicant credibly execute the proposed methods given their CV?
- Does the proposal naturally extend the professor's work, or does it merely cite it? A proposal that cites the professor's papers but does not build on their specific methods or open questions will not be compelling.
- Would the professor see a clear reason to supervise this project? The proposal should answer a question the professor has implicitly raised in their own work.

## Stress Test Protocol (Phase 2.8)

For the selected research direction, require the model to produce:

1. **Strongest selling point**: One sentence stating the most defensible and distinctive aspect of the proposal.
2. **Top 3–5 reviewer objections**: Drawn from the list above, specific to this proposal.
3. **Objection-neutralization strategy**: For each objection, state the exact revision needed to neutralize it — not a general response, but a specific change to the proposal text, scope, or methodology.

This output is internal. It informs the drafting of Phase 3 but does not appear verbatim in the final proposal.
