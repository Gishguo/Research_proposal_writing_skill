# **Research Proposal Template**

---

A research proposal is a document you submit before you begin your dissertation or thesis. Its purpose is to convince your supervisor, department, or ethics committee that your planned study is worthwhile, clearly defined, and realistically achievable. Think of it as a pitch for your research project. You are not just describing what you want to do. You are making a case for why it should be done and how you will do it.

A strong proposal demonstrates three things: that you understand the existing research in your area, that you have identified a genuine gap worth investigating, and that you have a credible plan for filling that gap. Always check your university's specific requirements before you begin, as the expected length, structure, and level of detail vary by institution and level of study.

---

## **Section 1: Introduction**

The introduction is where you position your study. By the end of this section, the reader should understand what you are researching, why it matters, and what you plan to investigate. It moves from broad context down to a sharply defined research focus.

This section typically covers four things: background and context, a problem statement, a research rationale, and your research aim, objectives, and questions.

---

### **Part 1: Background and Context**

### **What to Include**

Your goal here is to orient the reader. By the end of this part, someone who is not an expert in your topic should understand the field, the key concepts, and why the general area is worth studying. Start broad, then gradually narrow the focus towards your specific topic.

You should cover:

* The broader field or subject area your research sits within  
* How the topic has developed or why it has become relevant  
* Key terms or concepts the reader needs to understand your study  
* Any important industry, policy, or societal context that frames the issue

Define any specialist terms clearly. Do not assume prior knowledge.

### **Example**

*"In recent years, the impact of remote work on employee productivity and wellbeing has become a key area of interest in organisational research. Traditionally, workplace performance has been assessed based on physical presence and structured office environments. However, with the widespread adoption of remote and hybrid work models, new factors such as digital collaboration, work-life balance, and self-management have emerged as critical influences on employee outcomes.*

*One core concept in this discussion is productivity, which can be defined in various ways, including output per hour, task completion rates, or subjective self-reported efficiency. Another key term is work engagement, referring to an employee's level of enthusiasm, commitment, and absorption in their work."*

### **Tips**

* Start broad, then narrow. Think of it as a funnel moving towards your specific topic.  
* Only include background that is directly relevant to your research problem. This is not a general essay.  
* Use academic sources to support your claims from the start.  
* If you are still refining your topic, this resource may help: https://gradcoach.com/how-do-i-find-the-right-research-topic/

### 

### **Part 2: Problem Statement**

### **What to Include**

This is where you define the specific research problem your study will address. By the end of this part, the reader should understand exactly what is not yet known or not well understood in the existing literature.

You need to make the gap explicit. Do not rely on implication or assumption. State it directly.

This part has two steps:

**Step 1: State the gap.** Acknowledge what the existing research has established, then clearly identify what is missing, contested, or underexplored.

**Step 2: Justify the gap.** Explain why it matters that this gap exists. What are the academic or real-world consequences of it remaining unfilled?

### **Example**

*"While remote work has become standard practice across many industries, its impact on employee productivity and engagement remains unclear. Some studies suggest that remote work enhances productivity by reducing distractions and commute times, while others highlight challenges such as isolation, lack of supervision, and blurred work-life boundaries.*

*Despite growing interest in this topic, there is limited consensus on the specific factors that influence productivity and engagement in remote settings, particularly in knowledge-based roles. This gap is significant because organisations are making long-term decisions about remote work policies without a clear evidence base for how individual work arrangements affect employee outcomes."*

### **Tips**

* Be specific. A vague problem statement will weaken your entire proposal.  
* Avoid saying the topic is "interesting" or "important" without explaining why.  
* The gap you identify here must connect directly to your research aim in the next part.  
* For further guidance on tightening your problem statement: https://gradcoach.com/research-problem-statement/

---

### 

### **Part 3: Research Rationale**

### **What to Include**

Now that you have identified the problem, this part explains why your study is worthwhile. By the end, the reader should be convinced that your research is both relevant and necessary.

Focus on two types of value:

**Academic value:** How does your study contribute to the existing body of knowledge? Does it fill a gap, test a theory in a new context, or challenge an existing assumption?

**Practical value:** Who in the real world will benefit from your findings, and how? Think about practitioners, organisations, policymakers, or communities.

### **Example**

*"This study will add to the academic understanding of remote work by examining how different work arrangements impact productivity and engagement at an individual level. Existing research often focuses on broad organisational trends, leaving a gap in practical, employee-focused insights.*

*For practitioners such as business leaders, HR professionals, and policymakers, this research will offer evidence-based recommendations for optimising remote work strategies. Effective policies informed by this kind of research have the potential to improve engagement, reduce burnout, and support more sustainable ways of working."*

### **Tips**

* Connect your rationale directly back to the gap you identified. The rationale should feel like the natural consequence of the problem.  
* Be specific about who benefits and how. Vague claims like "this will help organisations" are not convincing.  
* You do not need to claim your study will transform the field. A focused, credible contribution is enough.

### **Part 4: Research Aim, Objectives, and Questions**

### **What to Include**

This part translates your problem into a clear plan of inquiry. By the end, the reader should see direct alignment between the problem, your aim, your objectives, and your research questions. If these four elements do not connect logically, your proposal will feel weak.

This is the "golden thread" of your research. For more on how this works: https://gradcoach.com/research-aims-objectives-questions/

**Research Aim**

Your aim is a single, high-level statement of what your study seeks to achieve overall.

*Example:* *"This study aims to examine how different remote work arrangements impact employee productivity and engagement among knowledge-based professionals."*

**Research Objectives**

Your objectives break the aim down into specific, actionable tasks. Each one should be SMART, meaning Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, and Time-bound.

*Example:*

1. *To review existing literature on remote work, productivity, and engagement by \[date\]*  
2. *To assess the relationship between different remote work models and employee productivity by \[date\]*  
3. *To evaluate the impact of remote work arrangements on employee engagement and wellbeing by \[date\]*  
4. *To develop evidence-based recommendations for organisations designing remote work policies by \[date\]*

**Research Questions**

Your research questions translate your objectives into answerable questions. They drive the rest of your dissertation from the literature review through to your conclusions.

*Example:*

1. *How do different remote work arrangements affect employee productivity?*  
2. *What impact does remote work have on employee engagement and wellbeing?*  
3. *How can organisations optimise remote work policies based on these findings?*

### 

### **Tips**

* Your aim, objectives, and questions must align tightly. Read them together and check that each one flows logically from the last.  
* Use precise action verbs in your objectives: "to assess", "to examine", "to identify", "to compare." Avoid vague verbs like "to look at" or "to explore."  
* Number your research questions. This makes them easy to reference throughout the proposal and the dissertation.

## **Section 2: Literature Review**

The literature review demonstrates that you understand the existing research in your area and can position your study within it. By the end of this section, the reader should see how your study builds on prior work and where it makes a new contribution.

This is not a summary exercise. It is an argument. Your job is to synthesise what others have found, identify where the evidence is incomplete or conflicting, and show how your study addresses that.

This section covers three parts: the theoretical framework, empirical research, and the research gap.

---

### **Part 1: Theoretical Framework**

### **What to Include**

Your theoretical framework anchors your study in established concepts and theories. By the end of this part, the reader should understand which ideas and frameworks guide your research and why you have chosen them.

You should:

* Identify the key theories or conceptual frameworks relevant to your study  
* Explain what each theory or concept means in plain language  
* Explain why these frameworks are appropriate for your research

Define all key constructs clearly and consistently. Use the same terminology throughout.

### **Tips**

* Do not simply list theories. Explain how each one relates to your research problem.  
* If there are competing theoretical perspectives, acknowledge them and explain which you have adopted and why.  
* Keep the focus tight. Only include frameworks that directly inform your study.

---

### 

### **Part 2: Empirical Research**

### **What to Include**

This part shows what has already been studied in your area. By the end, the reader should understand the main patterns, agreements, and disagreements in the literature.

Group studies by theme rather than listing them one by one. Look for patterns across studies: where do researchers agree? Where do findings conflict? What have most studies measured, and what have they ignored?

### **Tips**

* Synthesise, do not summarise. Summarising means describing what each study found. Synthesising means drawing connections across studies and building an argument.  
* Group studies into themes. For example, "Studies focusing on productivity outcomes" or "Research examining wellbeing in remote settings."  
* This is where many students lose marks. For guidance on avoiding common mistakes: https://gradcoach.com/the-literature-review-mistakes-that-cost-you-marks/

---

### **Part 3: Research Gap**

### **What to Include**

This is where you clearly position your contribution. By the end, the reader should understand exactly what is missing in the existing literature and how your study addresses that gap.

Your gap statement should follow directly from the empirical research you have just reviewed. Do not introduce a new gap here that was not signalled earlier.

### **Example**

*"While existing studies have explored remote work trends at an organisational level, limited attention has been given to how individual work structures influence productivity and engagement in knowledge-based roles. Most prior research relies on quantitative survey data, which may not fully capture the nuanced experiences of employees in remote environments. In addition, while existing frameworks explain workplace performance in traditional settings, their applicability to remote work remains underexplored. This study addresses this gap by examining how different remote work arrangements shape employee experiences using a qualitative approach."*

### **Tips**

* Be specific about the nature of the gap. Is it a geographical gap? A methodological gap? A gap in the population studied?  
* Connect the gap directly back to your research aim. The reader should feel that your study is the natural next step.  
* Avoid overclaiming. Your study contributes to filling a gap. It does not close it entirely.

## **Section 3: Methodology**

The methodology section explains exactly how you will answer your research questions. By the end, the reader should be confident that your approach is appropriate, logical, and feasible. This is where you demonstrate rigour.

This section covers four parts: your methodological approach, data collection method, sampling strategy, and data analysis plan.

---

### **Part 1: Methodological Approach**

### **What to Include**

This part defines your overall research approach. By the end, the reader should understand whether your study is qualitative, quantitative, or mixed methods, and why that choice is appropriate for your research questions.

### **Example**

*"This study will adopt a qualitative research approach to explore employee experiences in remote work settings. This approach is appropriate because the study focuses on understanding perceptions, behaviours, and context rather than measuring predefined variables."*

### **Tips**

* Always justify your choice. Do not just state your approach. Explain why it fits your research questions.  
* If you are unsure which approach suits your study: https://gradcoach.com/qualitative-vs-quantitative-research/

---

### 

### **Part 2: Data Collection**

### **What to Include**

This part explains how you will gather your data. By the end, the reader should understand what data you will collect, using which method, and why that method is appropriate.

Common data collection methods include interviews, surveys, observations, focus groups, and document analysis. Choose the method that best fits your research questions and approach.

### **Example**

*"Data will be collected through semi-structured interviews with professionals working in remote and hybrid roles. This method allows for in-depth exploration of individual experiences while maintaining enough consistency across participants to enable comparison."*

### **Tips**

* Be specific about the method and format. For example, "face-to-face interviews" or "online self-completion surveys" is more useful than simply "interviews" or "surveys."  
* Explain why this method suits your research questions. Do not just describe what you will do.

---

### **Part 3: Sampling Strategy**

### **What to Include**

This part explains who your participants are and how you will select them. By the end, the reader should understand the logic behind your sample and be aware of any limitations.

You should cover:

* Your sampling method (for example, purposive, random, convenience, or snowball sampling)  
* Who your participants will be and how many  
* How you will recruit them  
* Any known limitations of your sampling approach

### 

### **Example**

*"This study will use purposive sampling to select participants with direct experience in remote work environments. The sample will consist of between 50 and 100 knowledge-based professionals across industries, recruited through professional networks and online platforms. A potential limitation of this approach is self-selection bias, as individuals with strong opinions about remote work may be more likely to volunteer."*

### **Tips**

* Justify your sample size. Larger is not always better. The appropriate size depends on your approach and method.  
* Acknowledging limitations does not weaken your proposal. It shows you have thought critically about your design: https://gradcoach.com/research-limitations/

---

### **Part 4: Data Analysis**

### **What to Include**

This part explains how you will make sense of your data once you have collected it. By the end, the reader should understand the specific analysis method you will use, the steps involved, and why this method is appropriate.

### **Example**

*"This study will use thematic analysis to analyse interview data. Thematic analysis is appropriate because it allows for the identification of patterns and meanings within participant experiences without being restricted to a predefined coding framework.*

*The analysis process will follow these steps:*

1. *Transcribe all interviews and remove identifiable information*  
2. *Read and re-read the data to develop familiarity*  
3. *Code key ideas across the dataset*  
4. *Group related codes into broader themes*  
5. *Review and refine themes for consistency*  
6. *Interpret findings in relation to the existing literature"*

### 

### **Tips**

* Name your analysis method specifically rather than saying you will "analyse the data."  
* Walk the reader through the process step by step. This demonstrates that you understand what you are committing to.  
* Connect your analysis method back to your research approach. A qualitative study and thematic analysis fit together. A quantitative study and regression analysis fit together. Make sure these are consistent.

## **Section 4: Practicalities**

This section demonstrates that your study is realistic and manageable. By the end, the reader should be confident that you can complete the project within the required time and resources. Many proposals are strong conceptually but weak here. Do not neglect this section.

---

### **Part 1: Project Plan**

### **What to Include**

Your project plan shows how you will manage your time from start to finish. Present a clear, realistic timeline that maps each major activity to a specific period.

You can present this as a simple table or a written timeline. Include activities such as finalising your proposal, conducting your literature review, collecting data, analysing data, writing up, and submitting.

### **Tips**

* Build in buffer time. Most research takes longer than expected, particularly data collection.  
* Be realistic. A timeline that is too ambitious will raise doubts about your planning.  
* For guidance on building your dissertation plan: https://gradcoach.com/dissertation-plan/

---

### **Part 2: Budget**

### **What to Include**

If your research involves any costs, list them here. This is especially important if you are applying for funding or need institutional approval for expenditure.

Common costs to consider include transcription services, software licences, participant incentives, travel for data collection, and printing costs.

If your research involves no costs, state this briefly and explain why.

### 

### **Tips**

* Even a small or zero-cost study benefits from a brief budget statement. It shows you have thought through the practicalities.  
* Be specific about costs where you can. Estimated figures are fine at the proposal stage.

---

## 

## **Section 5: References and Appendices**

### **Reference List**

List all sources you have cited in your proposal using your university's required referencing format, such as APA, Harvard, or Chicago. Every claim you make that draws on existing research must be supported by a correctly formatted reference.

### **Tips**

* Use a reference manager such as Zotero or Mendeley to keep your references organised from the start. This will save significant time later.  
* Check your university's preferred referencing style before you begin writing.

### **Appendices**

Include any supporting materials that are too detailed to sit within the main body of the proposal. Common appendices include draft interview guides, sample survey questions, ethics approval documentation, or a detailed budget breakdown.

---

## **A Final Note on Your Proposal**

Your proposal's job is to convince, not just describe. Every section should contribute to a single, coherent argument: that your research problem is real, that your study is the right response to it, and that you have the knowledge and plan to carry it out.

Three principles will serve you well. First, clarity beats complexity. Write plainly and precisely. Second, alignment is everything. Your problem, aim, objectives, questions, and methodology must connect logically from start to finish. Third, justify every choice. Do not just state what you will do. Explain why.
